5 Key Investment Principles for Beginners

Published: March 12, 2025
8 min read
By PesaGuru Team
Fundamentals

Investing can seem intimidating, especially for beginners. However, understanding a few core principles can help you build a solid foundation for your investment journey. In this guide, we'll explore five key investment principles that every Kenyan investor should know.

1. Start Early and Be Consistent

The most powerful force in investing is compound interest, which Albert Einstein reportedly called "the eighth wonder of the world." The earlier you start investing, the more time your money has to grow.

Consider this example: If you invest KES 10,000 monthly from age 25 with an 8% annual return, by age 60 you'll have approximately KES 23.1 million. If you start at age 35 instead, you'll have only about KES 9.9 million – less than half the amount despite only starting 10 years later.

Consistency is equally important. Set up automatic transfers to your investment accounts to ensure you're consistently building your portfolio, regardless of market conditions.

2. Diversify Your Investments

Diversification is a risk management strategy that involves spreading your investments across various asset classes to reduce exposure to any single investment. In Kenya, you can diversify by investing in:

  • Stocks (Equities): Through the Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE)
  • Bonds: Government Treasury Bonds or corporate bonds
  • Money Market Funds: Low-risk investment vehicles
  • Real Estate: Either directly or through REITs
  • Saccos: Cooperative societies that offer dividend returns

Diversification helps protect your portfolio from significant losses when one sector or asset class underperforms. Remember the saying, "Don't put all your eggs in one basket."

3. Understand Risk vs. Return

All investments carry some level of risk, and generally, higher potential returns come with higher risks. Understanding this relationship is critical when building your investment portfolio.

Here's a simplified risk-return hierarchy for common Kenyan investments:

Investment Type Risk Level Potential Annual Return (%)
Money Market Funds Low 8-10%
Government Bonds Low to Medium 10-14%
Fixed Deposits Low 7-9%
Saccos Medium 10-14%
NSE Stocks (Blue Chips) Medium to High 10-20%
Real Estate Medium to High 8-15%
Small Cap Stocks High Variable (can be -20% to +40%)

Your risk tolerance depends on factors like your age, financial goals, income stability, and personal comfort level with market fluctuations. Younger investors can typically take on more risk, while those nearing retirement might prefer more conservative investments.

4. Keep Costs Low

Investment costs can significantly impact your returns over time. Common costs for Kenyan investors include:

  • Brokerage Fees: When buying or selling stocks (typically 1.5-2.1% in Kenya)
  • Management Fees: For mutual funds or unit trusts (1-3% annually)
  • Exit Fees: When selling certain investments before maturity
  • Annual Maintenance Fees: For some investment accounts

To minimize costs:

  • Compare fee structures across different brokerages and fund managers
  • Consider low-cost index funds if available
  • Hold investments for longer periods to reduce transaction costs
  • Avoid investments with high upfront or ongoing fees
"The ideal investment strategy for most Kenyans is to find low-cost investment vehicles, diversify appropriately based on your risk tolerance, and hold for the long term. Short-term trading typically benefits brokers more than investors due to transaction costs."

5. Invest for the Long Term

Successful investing requires patience. Short-term market fluctuations can be alarming, but historically, markets tend to rise over the long term. The NSE, despite periods of volatility, has delivered positive returns over longer timeframes.

Avoid these common mistakes:

  • Panic Selling: Selling investments when markets drop
  • Trying to Time the Market: Attempting to buy at the lowest point and sell at the highest
  • Chasing Past Performance: Investing in what performed well recently without analyzing future prospects

Instead, adopt a long-term perspective and resist the urge to react to short-term market movements. Studies consistently show that investors who stay invested through market cycles outperform those who try to time the market.

Next Steps: Creating Your Investment Plan

Now that you understand these key principles, it's time to create your personal investment plan:

  1. Assess your current financial situation (income, expenses, debt)
  2. Define your financial goals (retirement, education, home purchase)
  3. Determine your risk tolerance through our Risk Assessment Tool
  4. Create a diversified portfolio aligned with your goals and risk tolerance
  5. Set up automatic investments to ensure consistency
  6. Review and rebalance your portfolio periodically (1-2 times per year)

Remember, successful investing is a marathon, not a sprint. By following these five principles – starting early, diversifying, understanding risk, keeping costs low, and maintaining a long-term perspective – you'll be well on your way to building wealth and achieving your financial goals.

Stock Market (NSE)

Learn how to invest in the Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE), analyze stocks, and build a diversified portfolio.

A Central Depository System (CDS) account is required to buy and sell shares on the Nairobi Securities Exchange. Here's a step-by-step guide to opening one:

  1. Choose a licensed stockbroker: There are several licensed stockbrokers in Kenya, including Dyer & Blair, Faida Investment Bank, Kingdom Securities, and others.
  2. Gather required documents:
    • Original ID card or passport
    • Two passport-sized photos
    • KRA PIN certificate
    • Proof of address (utility bill or bank statement)
  3. Fill out the CDS account opening form: Your chosen stockbroker will provide this form. You'll need to fill it out completely and sign it.
  4. Submit documents and pay fees: There is usually a small account opening fee (typically KES 500-1,000).
  5. Receive your CDS account number: This usually takes 1-3 business days.
  6. Fund your account: Deposit money with your broker to begin trading.

Most brokers now offer online platforms where you can view your portfolio and place trade orders. Trading hours on the NSE are Monday to Friday, 9:30 AM to 3:00 PM, excluding public holidays.

Transaction costs typically include:

  • Brokerage commission: 1.5% - 2.1% of transaction value
  • NSE levy: 0.12%
  • CMA levy: 0.12%
  • CDSC levy: 0.08%
  • Investor Compensation Fund fee: 0.01%

Remember to factor these costs into your investment decisions, as they affect your overall returns.

The Nairobi Securities Exchange (NSE) has several indices that track different segments of the market:

Key NSE Indices

  • NSE 20 Share Index: Tracks the performance of 20 blue-chip companies selected based on market capitalization, liquidity, and sector representation.
  • NSE All Share Index (NASI): Measures the overall performance of all listed companies, providing a broader view of market movements.
  • NSE 25 Share Index: Represents 25 companies selected based on market capitalization, liquidity, and free float.

Market Segments

The NSE is divided into different segments:

  • Main Investment Market Segment (MIMS): For large, established companies meeting stringent listing requirements.
  • Alternative Investment Market Segment (AIMS): For small and medium-sized companies with less stringent listing requirements.
  • Growth Enterprise Market Segment (GEMS): For small and medium enterprises seeking to raise capital while benefiting from lower listing requirements and costs.

Industry Sectors

Companies listed on the NSE are categorized into various sectors:

  • Banking: E.g., KCB Group, Equity Group, NCBA
  • Telecommunications: E.g., Safaricom
  • Manufacturing & Allied: E.g., East African Breweries, BAT Kenya
  • Energy & Petroleum: E.g., KenGen, Kenya Power
  • Insurance: E.g., Jubilee Holdings, Britam
  • Investment: E.g., Centum Investment
  • Commercial & Services: E.g., Nation Media Group, Scangroup
  • Agricultural: E.g., Sasini, Kakuzi
  • Automobiles & Accessories: E.g., Car & General
  • Construction & Allied: E.g., Bamburi Cement
  • Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs): E.g., ILAM Fahari I-REIT
  • Exchange Traded Funds (ETFs): E.g., NewGold ETF

Understanding these indices and segments can help you develop a more targeted investment strategy and better diversify your portfolio across the Kenyan market.

Fundamental analysis involves evaluating a company's financial health and business prospects to determine if its stock is a good investment. Here are the key metrics to examine when analyzing NSE-listed companies:

Financial Ratios

  • Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio: Compares a company's share price to its earnings per share. The average P/E ratio for NSE companies ranges from 8-15, though this varies by sector.
  • Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio: Compares a company's market value to its book value. A P/B ratio under 1 might indicate an undervalued stock, but could also signal problems.
  • Dividend Yield: Annual dividends divided by share price. The NSE has traditionally had strong dividend-paying stocks, with some yielding 5-10%.
  • Return on Equity (ROE): Measures profitability by showing how much profit a company generates with shareholders' money.
  • Debt-to-Equity Ratio: Shows how much a company is financed through debt versus shareholder equity.

Company Fundamentals

  • Revenue Growth: Consistent growth in sales over time indicates a healthy business.
  • Profit Margins: Higher margins suggest efficient operations and pricing power.
  • Cash Flow: Strong, consistent cash flow is often more important than reported earnings.
  • Management Quality: Experienced, trustworthy management with clear strategic vision.
  • Competitive Advantage: Factors that give the company an edge over competitors.

Industry and Economic Analysis

  • Industry Trends: Understanding the growth prospects of the sector.
  • Regulatory Environment: Pending legislation or regulations that could impact the company.
  • Economic Factors: Interest rates, inflation, exchange rates, and GDP growth can all affect stock performance.

Where to Find Information

  • Company Financial Reports: Annual reports, half-year results, and quarterly reports are published on company websites and the NSE website.
  • NSE Website: Provides market data, company announcements, and regulatory filings.
  • Capital Markets Authority (CMA): Offers regulatory information and market reports.
  • Financial News: Business Daily, Standard, Nation, and other financial news sources cover NSE companies.
  • Brokerage Research: Many Kenyan stockbrokers publish research reports on listed companies.

Remember that fundamental analysis should be complemented with an understanding of market sentiment and technical factors. No single metric tells the complete story – always look at multiple indicators before making investment decisions.
